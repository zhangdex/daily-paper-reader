# From Dialogue to Execution: Mixture-of-Agents Assisted Interactive Planning for Behavior Tree-Based Long-Horizon Robot Execution 

Kanata Suzuki 1, Kazuki Hori 1, Haruka Miyoshi 3,4, Shuhei Kurita 2,3, and Tetsuya Ogata 1,2,3

Abstract — Interactive task planning with large language models (LLMs) enables robots to generate high-level action plans from natural language instructions. However, in long-horizon tasks, such approaches often require many questions, increasing user burden. Moreover, flat plan representations become difficult to manage as task complexity grows. We propose a framework that integrates Mixture-of-Agents (MoA)-based proxy answering into interactive planning and generates Behavior Trees (BTs) for structured long-term execution. The MoA consists of multiple LLM-based expert agents that answer general or domain-specific questions when possible, reducing unnecessary human intervention. The resulting BT hierarchi-cally represents task logic and enables retry mechanisms and dynamic switching among multiple robot policies. Experiments on cocktail-making tasks show that the proposed method reduces human response requirements by approximately 27% while maintaining structural and semantic similarity to fully human-answered BTs. Real-robot experiments on a smoothie-making task further demonstrate successful long-horizon exe-cution with adaptive policy switching and recovery from action failures. These results indicate that MoA-assisted interactive planning improves dialogue efficiency while preserving execu-tion quality in real-world robotic tasks. 

I. INTRODUCTION Execution of long-horizon tasks remains one of the funda-mental challenges in learning-based robotic control systems. Tasks composed of multiple sequential stages, including branching conditions and repeated procedures, are particu-larly difficult for current learning-based models that primar-ily rely on short-term memory mechanisms [1][2]. Recent ad-vances in Large Language Models (LLMs [3][4]) and Vision-Language Models (VLMs [5][6]) have enabled sophisti-cated natural language-based planning [7][8][9][10][11], and these approaches have been applied to address long-horizon robotic tasks. Among them, interactive task planning meth-ods [12][13][14] represent a promising direction. Interactive task planning refines task specifications and preconditions through question–answer interactions with a user, generating executable plans from abstract instructions. This paradigm enables the execution of multi-stage long-horizon tasks with-out requiring detailed manual task engineering in advance. However, interactive task planning introduces its own challenges. Second, conventional LLM-based planning approaches often represent plans in tabular formats, which become increas-ingly verbose and difficult to manage as task complexity grows. Similarly, imitation learning approaches that take language as input [15][16][17], as well as Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models [18][19], typically accept only few instructions at a time. Although recent studies have incorpo-rated natural language-based subtask decomposition into the learning process [19], the representational capacity remains limited due to token-length constraints. To address the first limitation, we integrate a Mixture of Agents (MoA) mechanism into interactive task plan-ning, enabling proxy responses within the dialogue process (Fig. 1). By delegating questions that can be answered using general knowledge to the LLM agents themselves, the system reduces the frequency of human intervention while maintaining efficient refinement of task specifications. To address the second limitation, we introduce Behavior Trees (BTs [20][21]) as the task representation format. BTs allow complex action logic to be described hierarchically and modularly, making them particularly suitable for repre-senting long-horizon tasks that involve repeated operations and conditional branching. In our experiments, we examine whether the robot can successfully execute the entire long-horizon task by dynamically switching learned action models according to the generated BT structure. II. RELATED WORK Several studies have explored the use of LLMs to generate task plans for robots. These approaches can be broadly categorized into offline task planning, where a complete 

> arXiv:2603.01113v1 [cs.RO] 1 Mar 2026

task plan is generated prior to execution [7][8][9][10], and online task planning, where plans are continuously revised during execution [22][23][11]. One of the most well-known offline planning approaches is SayCan [7], which learns an action selection scheme in advance and grounds language instructions to robot actions through reinforcement learning in real environments. And, Liu et al. proposed improving the feasibility of LLM-generated task predictions by incor-porating scene graphs of the environment [24]. Other studies have reported the generation of BTs through hierarchical or stepwise planning strategies [25][26]. There are also approaches that enhance long-horizon planning capability by integrating classical planners during intermediate planning stages [27]. While these methods are promising in that they can directly reflect human intent into task plans, they typically require detailed and explicit task specifications to be provided as prompts in advance. When considering general users without domain expertise, manually specifying detailed prompts is often impractical. To alleviate this burden, interactive task planning has been proposed, in which an LLM generates questions to humans and iteratively fills in missing information through dialogue [12][13][14]. Ren et al. investigated active correc-tion mechanisms for online sequential task planning [13]. They evaluated the uncertainty of task plans based on natural language instructions from humans and visual observations from the robot, clarifying the plan by selectively generating questions in response to uncertainty. Hori et al. proposed allowing the LLM itself to analyze uncertainty in the gen-erated action plan and to generate corrective questions for segments deemed highly uncertain [14]. The key advantage of interactive planning is that the necessary information is obtained through dialogue, enabling the construction of plans without requiring detailed task descriptions upfront. However, interactive task planning also presents chal-lenges. LLM planners may generate a large number of clarification questions to compensate for missing informa-tion, some of which are redundant or non-essential for task execution. For example, in a scrambled egg cooking task, the system may ask about the “type of eggs to use,” a detail that is typically inconsequential in a standard household environment [14]. Such redundant questions increase the number of human–robot interactions and consequently raise operational costs in practical deployment. Moreover, many previous experimental validations have focused on short-horizon tasks using real robots [13]. As a result, it remains unclear to what extent action plans generated through in-teractive planning can robustly support long-horizon task execution on physical robots. III. PROPOSED METHOD In this study, we propose a framework that executes long-horizon tasks on a real robot by utilizing a Behavior Tree generated through interactive task planning, while dynami-cally switching between multiple motion generation models. The proposed framework consists of two main components:  

> •

BT generation through interactive action planning aug-mented with a Mixture of Agents (MoA),  

> •

Long-term task execution using a motion generation model attached to BT’s action nodes. The former aims to reduce the cost of question–answer interactions during planning, while the latter evaluates the feasibility of executing generated plans in real environments. 

A. BT Generation via MoA-Based Interactive Task Planning 

Figure 2 illustrates the overall processing flow of the proposed framework. The method takes natural language task instructions as input and progressively refines task specifica-tions through dialogue, ultimately generating an executable BT. First, the LLM planner makes BT from the provided natural language instruction, and analyzes uncertainty in the BT. For interactive planning, we adopt the method proposed in [14]. Through uncertainty analysis, information necessary for task execution but not explicitly stated in the instruction is extracted, and clarification questions are generated to sup-plement missing details. By responding to these questions, progressively filling in the gaps in the task plan generated by the LLM. By iterating this dialogue process as needed, task specifications are gradually concretized, and a BT represent-ing the entire task is generated. The resulting BT is described in XML format and structured as an executable task plan that includes conditional branches and retry mechanisms. For detailed prompt design, please refer to [14]. 

1) Proxy Response Mechanism via MoA: In the proposed framework, responses to generated questions are supported not only by direct user input but also by proxy responses through MoA. It has been shown that leveraging the com-monsense knowledge embedded in LLMs as agents can facilitate complex task execution [28][29]. Our MoA frame-work consists of multiple LLM-based agents with distinct perspectives, such as robot operational constraints, task spec-ifications, and commonsense reasoning. Each agent generates candidate answers to a given question, and the final response is determined by integrating these outputs. Each proxy-response agent follows a shared processing based on the given questions and task prerequisites. Robot Expert The robot expert is responsible for answer-ing questions related to the robot’s motion capabilities, exe-cutable actions, and sensor configuration. In our experiments, this agent only responds to questions strictly concerning the robot’s functional specifications and outputs unanswered results for uncertain or out-of-scope queries. 

Task Domain Expert The task domain expert supple-ments procedural knowledge specific to the target task. In experiment 1 described later, which involves beverage preparation, we employed an agent with domain knowledge corresponding to bartending operations. However, the frame-work itself is domain-agnostic and can be extended to other task domains (experiment 2). 

Commonsense Expert The commonsense expert handles questions that rely on general knowledge or everyday rea-soning rather than specialized expertise. When the answer depends heavily on task context or involves ambiguous judgment, the agent outputs the question as unanswered. The purpose of MoA-based proxy responses is not to completely replace human input, but rather to serve as an auxiliary mechanism that facilitates smoother interactive planning. Specifically, it plays the following roles:  

> •

Supplementing information that the user may not know,  

> •

Internally resolving questions that can be inferred from manuals or general knowledge,  

> •

Handling redundant questions that do not strictly require human responses. By doing so, the framework maintains the quality of dialogue necessary for refining task specifications while reducing the number of interactions and cognitive burden on the user. 

B. Task Execution on a Real Robot Using the Generated BT 

The generated BT includes condition nodes and action nodes. The former describes conditional branching, and the latter describes specific actions. Condition nodes and action nodes are explicitly separated, enabling systematic management of state transitions across task stages. In our implementation, we assign an individual imitation learning model to each action node in the BT. In this study, we used Diffusion Policy [2] and π0.5 [19], which are representative imitation learning models. Because BT explicitly represents task stages as nodes, it is well-suited for long-horizon task execution involving such model switching. For action nodes that do not involve physical actions of the robot, no model is assigned. In addition, in condition nodes that handle conditional branching, the success or failure of each action is evaluated by sending images before and after the execution of the subtask to VLM [5]. With such an allocation, the system dynamically switches between multiple motion generation models depending on the required action characteristics. BT-based execution en-ables robust task progression: even if certain actions fail, retry mechanisms or transitions to subsequent stages can be handled systematically, ensuring overall task robustness. Using this framework, we evaluate whether a BT generated through interactive action planning can function effectively as a long-horizon executable plan on a physical robot in a real-world environment. IV. EXPERIMENTS To evaluate the quality of BTs generated through the proposed method, we conducted two types of experiments: (1) a quantitative evaluation of generated BTs using defined metrics, and (2) a long-horizon task execution experiment on a real robot using the generated BTs. 

A. Experiment 1 

First, we compare the BTs generated with and without the proposed proxy-response mechanism in terms of structural and semantic similarity using two evaluation metrics. This experiment is positioned as an exploratory evaluation rather than a statistical significance test or a claim of generalization performance. Its purpose is to qualitatively and quantitatively analyze how the introduction of proxy responses affects the characteristics of generated BTs. As the experimental task, we selected a cocktail prepa-ration task: “Make a Margarita.” The LLM was given the abstract instruction: “Make a cocktail.” Through interactive Blender

At the beginning of the task, neither the concrete procedure nor the required tools are explicitly specified. Therefore, an executable BT cannot be constructed without clarifying the task specification through dialogue. This task naturally increases the number of question–answer interactions while maintaining an appropriate level of com-plexity for observing the behavior of both interactive plan-ning and proxy responses. For this task, BTs were generated under the following two conditions:  

> •

Without MoA : All questions generated during the dialogue were answered by a human.  

> •

With MoA : Questions were answered using MoA-based proxy responses in addition to human input, reducing the human response burden. For each condition, BT generation was performed three times, resulting in six BTs in total for evaluation. We used Gemini 2.0 Flash [5] as the LLM for BT generation, with the temperature set to 1.0 to introduce diversity in generation outcomes. This setting allows us to compare the impact of proxy responses under stochastic generation conditions. 

Metric 1: To evaluate structural similarity between gen-erated BTs, we employed the Tree Edit Distance (TED). TED is defined as the minimum number of edit operations required to transform one tree into another. Edit operations include node insertion, deletion, and substitution, directly capturing differences in hierarchical structure. In this study, we adopted the TED definition proposed by Zhang et al. [30] and employed the normalization method proposed by Li et al. [31], which addresses cases where the triangle inequality does not hold. The normalized TED is defined as: 

dN-TED (T1, T 2) = 2 · TED( T1, T 2)

α(|T1| + |T2|) + TED( T1, T 2) (1) where T1 and T2 denote the BTs being compared, |T |

represents the number of nodes in the tree, and α denotes the maximum cost of edit operations. In this experiment, all edit operations were assigned a cost of 1. The normalized TED takes values in [0 , 1] , where smaller values indicate greater structural similarity. 

Metric 2: To evaluate semantic similarity between BTs, we measured embedding-based similarity between nodes. Each BT node was extracted as a natural language expression and converted into a fixed-length embedding vector using Sentence-BERT [32]. For each node in one BT, cosine similarity was calculated against all nodes in the other BT, and the maximum similarity value was taken as the corre-spondence score. The average of these maximum similarities over all nodes was then computed as the overall semantic similarity between the two BTs. This metric takes values in 

[0 , 1] , where larger values indicate higher semantic similarity.

VI. CONCLUSION This study presented a framework for long-horizon robot task execution that combines interactive LLM-based plan-ning, MoA answering, and BT generation. By integrating MoA into the interactive planning process, the system was able to reduce human response requirements while preserv-ing the structural and semantic quality of generated plans. Quantitative evaluation showed that the generated BTs re-mained comparable to fully human-answered plans in terms of both normalized Tree Edit Distance and embedding-based similarity. Furthermore, real-robot experiments demonstrated that the generated BTs could be directly executed in a phys-ical environment. By assigning different imitation learning models to individual action nodes and leveraging the retry structures inherent in BTs, the robot successfully performed a multi-step smoothie-making task, even under occasional execution failures. These findings indicate that structured BT representations combined with adaptive model switching are effective for robust long-term task execution. Future work includes scaling the MoA framework to broader task domains [34] and incorporating automatic agent selection strategies.
