> MICHELE CIRCELLI
> Abstract. We study a stochastic variant of the Team Orienteering Problem (TOP) with uncertain travel times and an all-or-nothing reward policy, under which the reward of a route is lost if its travel time exceeds the available budget. This setting makes the trade-off between expected reward and route reliability a central issue in solution design. To address this problem, we propose a reliability-aware simheuristic that combines a savings-based con-structive heuristic, controlled randomization, local search, and Monte Carlo simulation. The method evaluates candidate solutions directly under uncertainty and selects them using both estimated expected reward and a reliability criterion, rather than relying on deterministic optimization followed by ex-post stochastic evaluation. Computational experiments on benchmark instances adapted from the TOP literature show that the pro-posed approach substantially improves stochastic performance with respect to a deterministic baseline evaluated under uncertainty. In most instances, the simheuristic increases both expected reward and reliability, and in the loosest regimes reliability can approach 0.99 while keeping computation times moderate.

Introduction 

The Team Orienteering Problem (TOP) is a well-known variant of vehicle routing problems in which a fleet of vehicles must select and visit a subset of customers or sites within a limited time or distance budget, with the objective of maximizing the total collected reward. Each location can be visited at most once, and not all locations need to be served. Due to these characteristics, the TOP has been successfully applied to a wide range of real-world contexts, including service routing, surveillance operations, inspection planning, and related routing applications [5, 15, 3]. In many practical settings, however, travel times are subject to uncertainty due to factors such as traffic congestion, weather conditions, terrain characteristics, or operational disruptions. This motivates the study of the Stochastic Team Orienteering Problem (STOP), where travel times are modeled as random variables. In this setting, solutions must not only maximize the expected reward but also account for the risk of violating route-duration constraints. As a consequence, deterministic solutions that appear attractive under nominal conditions may become fragile when evaluated in stochastic environments, showing a substantial loss in both expected reward and reliability [4, 17]. Despite its practical relevance, the stochastic version of the TOP has received much less attention than its deterministic counterpart. Moreover, although simheuristic ideas have already been explored in routing and orienteering under uncertainty, fewer works focus on multi-vehicle TOP settings with route failure risk under an all-or-nothing reward structure. In this setting, solution quality cannot be assessed only through nominal collected reward, because a route that is attractive in deterministic terms may become fragile once travel-time uncertainty is taken into account. In this paper, we address this issue by proposing a reliability-aware simheuristic for a stochastic TOP with lognormally distributed travel times and all-or-nothing route rewards. The method combines a savings-based construction procedure with controlled randomization, local search, and Monte Carlo simulation. Random-ization is introduced to diversify the candidate solutions generated by the constructive and improvement phases, while simulation is used to estimate the stochastic performance of each candidate directly during the search. A distinctive feature of the proposed framework is that candidate solutions are selected under uncertainty using both estimated expected reward and a reliability requirement. Hence, the method does not simply construct deterministic solutions and evaluate them ex post, but incorporates stochastic information into the search and selection process itself. In addition, the savings parameter is screened in the stochastic setting rather than being fixed only according to deterministic reward, since a value that is attractive under nominal travel times need not remain attractive once uncertainty is considered.

The proposed approach is evaluated on benchmark instances adapted from the TOP literature and extended to incorporate stochastic travel times. The computational study shows that embedding stochastic evaluation within the search leads to solutions that are consistently more robust than those produced by a deterministic heuristic evaluated under uncertainty. In particular, the simheuristic often achieves higher expected reward together with markedly higher reliability, and in the loosest regimes the estimated reliability can approach 0.99. The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows. First, we study a stochastic variant of the Team Orienteering Problem with uncertain travel times and an all-or-nothing reward policy, where reliability becomes a central performance dimension together with expected reward. Second, we propose a reliability-aware simheuristic that combines savings-based construction, controlled randomization, local search, and Monte Carlo simulation in a unified search framework. Third, the method evaluates and selects candidate solutions directly under uncertainty, using a reliability threshold together with estimated expected reward instead of relying on deterministic optimization followed by ex-post stochastic assessment. Fourth, the computational analysis shows that this approach can substantially improve stochastic solution quality over a deterministic baseline, often with large gains in both expected reward and reliability. Section 1 presents the mathematical model. Section 2 describes the proposed algorithmic framework. Section 3 reports the computational experiments and discusses the results. Finally, Section 4 concludes the paper and outlines future research directions. 

Literature Review. The Orienteering Problem (OP) is a classical routing problem in which a vehicle must select a subset of customers to visit within a limited time or distance budget in order to maximize the total collected reward. Since its introduction by Golden et al. [10], the OP and its variants have attracted substantial attention in the operations research literature. Broad surveys can be found in Vansteenwegen et al. [28] and Gunawan et al. [15], while C´ aceres-Cruz et al. [3] discuss the OP within the wider class of rich vehicle routing problems. Classical heuristic ideas for reward-oriented routing were also developed early in the orienteering literature; in particular, Chao et al. [6] proposed a fast and effective heuristic for the single-vehicle Orienteering Problem. The Team Orienteering Problem (TOP), introduced by Chao et al. [5], extends the OP to the case of multiple vehicles, which must collectively maximize the total reward gathered from visiting a subset of nodes. Because the TOP is NP-hard, exact methods are generally limited to medium-sized instances. For example, Butt and Ryan [2] proposed a column-generation approach capable of solving moderate-size problems. For larger instances, the literature has mainly focused on heuristic and metaheuristic methods. A wide range of metaheuristics has been proposed for the deterministic TOP. Archetti et al. [1] developed tabu search and variable neighborhood search procedures, showing the effectiveness of neighborhood-based improvement strategies. Tang and Miller-Hooks [27] proposed a tabu search heuristic, while Dang et al. [7] introduced a particle-swarm-optimization-inspired algorithm. Multi-start simulated annealing was studied by Lin [20], and evolutionary approaches were explored, among others, by Ferreira et al. [9] and Ke et al. [18]. Overall, these contributions show that combining diversification and intensification mechanisms is crucial to effectively explore the TOP solution space and obtain high-quality solutions. Compared with the deterministic TOP, stochastic variants have received much less attention. In many real applications, travel and service times are uncertain, which may cause routes planned under nominal conditions to become infeasible in practice. This issue is especially relevant in applications such as surveillance, inspection, emergency response, and defense-related missions, where route failure may have severe operational consequences. Early work on stochastic orienteering mainly focused on the single-vehicle setting. Ilhan et al. [16], for instance, considered uncertainty in rewards, while Campbell et al. [4], Papapanagiotou et al. [24], Evers et al. [8], and Verbeeck et al. [30] studied variants with stochastic travel and/or service times. These works differ in how uncertainty is modeled and in how route violations are handled, ranging from recourse and penalty-based mechanisms to chance-constrained formulations and stricter feasibility policies [26, 19, 29, 31]. However, most of this literature remains centered on the single-tour problem, and extensions to the multi-vehicle case are still comparatively scarce. To cope with uncertainty in combinatorial optimization, simheuristics have emerged as a promising family of methods. Simheuristics combine metaheuristic search with simulation techniques, typically Monte Carlo RELIABILITY-AWARE SIMHEURISTIC FOR THE STOCHASTIC TEAM ORIENTEERING PROBLEM 3

simulation, in order to evaluate and compare candidate solutions under stochastic conditions. Juan et al. [17] provide a comprehensive review of simheuristics and discuss their flexibility in problems where analytical evaluation is difficult or computationally prohibitive. Simheuristic ideas have already proved effective in several routing settings, including stochastic inventory routing [14], stochastic waste collection [13], and stochastic arc routing [11]. These studies show that integrating simulation inside the optimization loop can substantially improve the quality of decisions in uncertain environments. Recent work by Juan and co-authors has also provided evidence of the value of simulation-based opti-mization and biased randomization in routing and orienteering-related problems. In particular, simheuristic approaches have already been proposed for the stochastic team orienteering problem, showing that the ex-plicit integration of Monte Carlo simulation into the search can improve decision quality under uncertain travel times [22, 23]. In parallel, biased-randomized strategies have been successfully applied to TOP-related settings, including real-time solution methods and dynamic-reward variants, confirming the use-fulness of combining fast constructive procedures with diversification mechanisms in reward-driven routing problems [21, 25, 12]. Within this context, biased randomization has also attracted growing interest as a way to enhance the exploratory ability of constructive heuristics while preserving their computational efficiency. Grasas et al. [12] show that skewed probability distributions can be used to relax the greedy behavior of classical heuristics and generate diversified high-quality solutions. This idea is particularly appealing in stochastic routing, where diversification is not only useful for escaping poor local choices but can also improve robustness under uncertainty. Although previous work has shown the value of simulation-based optimization and biased randomization in routing, the specific combination of these ideas in stochastic multi-vehicle orienteering settings still leaves room for further investigation. In particular, relatively less attention has been devoted to settings in which route failure has an all-or-nothing effect on collected reward and where solution selection must explicitly account for both expected reward and reliability. This paper builds on the existing simheuristic literature rather than replacing it. Our contribution is to focus on a stochastic TOP setting with all-or-nothing route rewards and to propose a reliability-aware search framework in which: (i) candidate solutions are generated through a savings-based heuristic enriched with controlled randomization and local search; (ii) candidate values of the savings parameter are screened directly under uncertainty; and (iii) the final solution is selected by combining estimated expected reward with a reliability requirement. In this sense, the paper contributes a specific algorithmic design and an empirical assessment of its behavior on benchmark instances adapted to stochastic travel times. 

Problem statement. We consider a routing problem in which a fleet of m vehicles must visit a subset of geographically dispersed customers or sites within a limited operational time budget. This setting naturally arises in several contexts, including distribution planning, field service operations, surveillance missions, inspection activities, and defense-related tasks. Each vehicle operates within a single mission or working shift and is subject to a maximum route-duration limit Tmax > 0. Since this budget is typically restrictive, not all customers or sites can be visited within the same planning horizon, and a selection must therefore be made. Each node i is associated with a nonnegative reward ui ≥ 0, representing the value, priority, or urgency of visiting that location. Each location can be visited at most once across the entire fleet, so that its reward can be collected only once. Every vehicle must depart from a designated start depot and terminate its route at a designated end depot. The objective is to determine a set of at most m start-to-end routes that maximizes the total collected reward. In the deterministic setting, a route is considered feasible if its total travel time does not exceed 

Tmax . In the stochastic setting, however, route duration is uncertain. Following an all-or-nothing policy, if the realized travel time of a vehicle exceeds Tmax , the vehicle is assumed to fail to complete its route and all rewards associated with that route are lost. Hence, uncertainty directly affects not only the ability of a route to be completed within the available time budget, but also the expected value of a solution. We study two versions of the problem. In the deterministic version, travel times are approximated by the Euclidean distances between locations. In the stochastic version, travel times are modeled as random variables following log-normal distributions, in order to capture uncertainty due to factors such as traffic conditions, weather, terrain characteristics, or operational disruptions. 4 MICHELE CIRCELLI 

Therefore, while the deterministic problem aims at maximizing the total collected reward over feasible routes under nominal travel times, the stochastic problem aims at maximizing expected collected reward under uncertain travel times, with solution reliability playing a central role in the evaluation and selection of candidate solutions within the algorithmic framework. 1. 2. Algorithms 

This section describes the algorithmic framework adopted to compute solutions for both the deterministic and the stochastic versions of the problem. We follow a constructive-first approach: a fast heuristic builds, for each vehicle, a deterministic-time-feasible start-to-end route as an ordered sequence of visited nodes, and the resulting solution is then refined by local improvement moves (e.g., intra-route 2-opt, greedy reinsertion of unvisited nodes, and replacement moves of the form visited ↔ unvisited). In the stochastic extension, solutions are still constructed as deterministic-time-feasible routes, but they are evaluated under random travel times through Monte Carlo simulation. Therefore, although the stochastic formulation does not impose (3) as a hard feasibility constraint, the proposed heuristic restricts the search to this nominally feasible subset for algorithmic simplicity and solution robustness. 2.1. [23], adapted to the start-to-end case. The construction is followed by an intra-route 2-opt improvement. We then apply a greedy reinsertion procedure to exploit any remaining slack in the time budgets by inserting additional unvisited nodes. Finally, we apply a replacement step based on moves of the form visited ↔ unvisited, which can improve the total reward even when no further feasible insertions exist. To handle uncertainty, we adopt a simheuristic approach, i.e., we combine a randomized heuristic search with Monte Carlo simulation in order to estimate the stochastic objective and select robust solutions. 2.1.6. Randomized multi-start search. Let A ⊂ (0 , 1) denote the set of candidate values for the savings parameter α. If grid search is disabled, then A = {α}; otherwise, A is a small grid of values. Unlike the deterministic setting, where α is selected according to the final deterministic reward, in the stochastic setting candidate values of α are evaluated directly under uncertainty. This distinction is important because the deterministic criterion and the stochastic criterion are not the same: a value of α that performs best under nominal travel times does not necessarily remain the best when solutions are compared in terms of expected reward and reliability. For each α ∈ A , we generate K candidate solutions by running a randomized variant of the deterministic pipeline. For a given pair ( α, q ), the candidate ρ(α,q ) is built as follows: (1) randomized savings-based construction with Top-Ltop selection in the merge phase; (2) deterministic intra-route 2-opt improvement; (3) randomized reinsertion of unvisited nodes with Top-Ltop selection; (4) randomized replacement moves of the form visited ↔ unvisited with Top-Ltop selection; (5) final deterministic intra-route 2-opt cleanup. Hence, the simheuristic introduces randomization in three phases: savings-based construction, reinsertion, and replacement, while the intra-route 2-opt steps remain deterministic. The local-improvement phases are not merely a post-processing step. The first intra-route 2-opt improves route structure before the stochastic evaluation, while reinsertion and replacement exploit residual slack to increase collected reward; the final 2-opt cleanup further shortens the randomized routes and can therefore contribute to robustness by increasing time slack. 2.1.7. Overall performance on the full dataset. Across the dataset, the simheuristic improves the es-timated expected reward in almost all cases and improves reliability in almost all cases as well; the only null-improvement cases are the very tight instances in which the two methods return the same solution. Overall, the results indicate a clear advantage of embedding stochastic evaluation within the search. 3.2. Effect of the fleet size and time budget. The strongest gains are observed in the p4.2.* family (m = 2), where the simheuristic improves the expected reward on every tested instance. In the loosest instances, the simheuristic pushes reliability close to one, reaching values around 0 .99, which indicates that the probability of route completion can become almost certain in the most favorable regimes. The p4.3.* family ( m = 3) shows the same pattern, with gains that become particularly pronounced in the medium and loose regimes. For the p4.4.* family ( m = 4), the improvements remain clearly positive on almost all instances, although somewhat smaller on average. In general, the benefit of the simheuristic is weakest only in the tightest cases, where the feasible search space is extremely restricted and both methods may converge to the same solution. 3.3. Solution structure and trade-offs. A recurrent pattern in the tables is that the simheuristic often attains a much larger bE[Reward] while visiting fewer nodes and sometimes collecting a slightly lower de-terministic reward. This behavior is consistent with the all-or-nothing route payoff: under uncertain travel times, a solution with a slightly smaller deterministic reward can still be preferable if it is much more likely to remain within the time budget. Hence, the simheuristic is not simply producing “larger” deterministic solutions; rather, it is reshaping routes so as to balance collected reward and completion probability. This also explains the use of a reliability-aware selection policy: under an all-or-nothing payoff structure, prioritizing reliability can be essential to preserve expected reward. 3.4. Computational effort. As expected, the deterministic baseline remains very fast, whereas the simheuris-tic is significantly more expensive because it combines randomized multi-start search with repeated Monte Carlo evaluation. The computational burden grows with instance difficulty and with the richness of the feasible search space. Therefore, the simheuristic should be seen as a more expensive but substantially more robust optimization procedure: it trades additional computation for markedly better expected performance under uncertainty. 3.5. Main takeaway. The experiments on the full dataset confirm a clear message: optimizing only the deterministic reward can be misleading under uncertain travel times. The simheuristic consistently finds solutions that are more reliable and, as a consequence, achieve much higher expected reward. This advantage is especially strong in the more challenging instances, but it remains visible across almost the entire benchmark set. 4. Conclusions 

This paper has addressed a stochastic variant of the Team Orienteering Problem in which travel times are uncertain and route completion follows an all-or-nothing reward policy. In this setting, solutions must balance the objective of collecting high reward with the need to remain reliable under uncertainty, since route failures may cause the complete loss of the reward associated with a route. To tackle this problem, we proposed a simheuristic framework that combines a savings-based constructive heuristic with controlled randomization, local search, and Monte Carlo simulation. The method evaluates candidate solutions directly under stochastic travel times and selects them according to both expected reward and reliability. In particular, randomized construction and improvement phases are used to diversify the search, while the local search components refine route structure and may increase time slack. The computational results on benchmark instances show that explicitly incorporating uncertainty into the search leads to solutions that are substantially more robust than those obtained by deterministic optimization followed by ex-post stochastic evaluation. In most cases, the proposed simheuristic achieves higher expected reward together with significantly improved reliability, and in the loosest regimes reliability can become very close to one. These results also confirm that, under an all-or-nothing reward policy, reliability is not merely an auxiliary indicator, but a central decision criterion. Overall, the proposed approach provides an effective trade-off between robustness and computational effort. Although more expensive than the deterministic baseline, it remains computationally manageable and is able to generate high-quality solutions within moderate running times. Several directions remain open for future research. These include testing alternative reliability measures and selection policies, analyzing the sensitivity of the method to different variability levels, and extending the framework to richer versions of the problem with additional operational constraints or alternative uncertainty structures. Since the final intra-route 2-opt cleanup tends to shorten routes and increase time slack, it may also remain useful under higher variability levels; however, this should be assessed explicitly in future computational tests. Future research could also investigate the sensitivity of the method to the main algorithmic parameters, such as the number of starts, the Monte Carlo sample size, the Top-Ltop parameter, and the reliability threshold. A more systematic calibration of these parameters, or their adaptive adjustment during the search, could further improve the trade-off between expected reward, reliability, and computational time.
